1. Proof of Ownership:

• Deed (Grant Deed, Warranty Deed, etc.): This is the most fundamental document proving you legally own the property. Make sure it's the most recent deed and that your name(s) and the property description match exactly. • Title Insurance Policy: While not strictly required to sell, having a copy of your title insurance policy can be helpful. It shows you've already had the title examined and insured. It can expedite the buyer's title search.

2. Identification:

• Government-Issued Photo ID (Driver's License, Passport, etc.): Required to verify your identity at various stages of the transaction (e.g., when signing documents).

3. Loan Information (If Applicable):

• Mortgage Statement: The most recent mortgage statement, showing your current loan balance, interest rate, monthly payment, and lender contact information. • Payoff Statement: Obtain a formal payoff statement from your lender. This is an official document that details the exact amount required to pay off your mortgage in full on a specific date. It often includes prepayment penalties (if any) and per diem interest.

4. Property Disclosures

These are legal documents where you disclose any known material defects or issues with the property. Disclosure requirements vary significantly by location. Common examples include: • Seller's Property Disclosure Statement (or similar): A form where you list any known problems with the property, such as leaks, structural issues, appliance malfunctions, pest infestations, etc. • Lead-Based Paint Disclosure (for properties built before 1978): A federal requirement. You must disclose any known lead-based paint hazards and provide buyers with an EPA-approved pamphlet. • Natural Hazard Disclosure Statement (in some states like California): Discloses if the property is in a flood zone, earthquake fault zone, fire hazard zone, etc. • Environmental Hazards Disclosure: Disclosures related to radon, asbestos, mold, underground storage tanks, etc. • Megan's Law Disclosure (in some states): Informs buyers that they can access information about registered sex offenders in the area.

5. Homeowners Association (HOA) Documents (If Applicable):

• HOA Governing Documents: These include the HOA's bylaws, covenants, conditions, and restrictions (CC&Rs), rules and regulations, and articles of incorporation. • HOA Financial Statements: Balance sheets, income statements, and reserve studies. • HOA Resale Package (or similar): A package of documents specifically prepared for the sale of a property within the HOA. It typically includes the governing documents, financial statements, and information about assessments, fees, and insurance. • HOA Contact Information: Contact information for the HOA management company or board members.

6. Property Information

• Survey Plat: A map showing the property's boundaries, dimensions, and any easements or encroachments. • Property Tax Bills: Recent property tax bills. • Homeowners Insurance Policy: Your current homeowners insurance policy. • Appraisal Report (If Available): If you've had a recent appraisal done, it can be helpful to provide a copy to potential buyers. • Home Inspection Reports (If Available): If you've had a pre-listing home inspection, you may want to provide a copy. This can increase buyer confidence, but be aware you'll likely have to disclose any issues it reveals. • List of Fixtures and Personal Property: A clear list of what is included in the sale (e.g., appliances, window coverings, light fixtures) and what is excluded.
